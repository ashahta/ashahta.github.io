Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Full disclosure: I've been really excited about touching on this topic, because it means a lot to me and now I finally get to talk about it, so please bear with me when you read through the post. I've done my best to keep things brief, but not too brief.

“Neuroplasticity [mass noun] The ability of the brain to form and reorganize synaptic connections, especially in response to learning or experience or following injury.” Oxford's Lexico

Neuroplasticity, is the ability of the brain to change and grow throughout one's life. It is responsible for the changes that take place when learning to play a musical instrument, speaking a new language, recovering from an injury, as well as creating new ways of functioning and new behaviours/habits for those with behavioural and mental disabilities. Neuroplasticity isn't anything new. It's been touched on in studies as far back as the late 1700's, (but in animals), and then again in 1890, (with the initial term plasticity) and then neglected and forgotten for nearly another century as neuroscientists believed that the growth of the human brain and function was fixed in adulthood.

Contrary to that belief however, the evidence around the 'rewiring' of neural pathways has been sufficiently proven, albeit indirectly, through patients who have suffered strokes, people with mental health issues, through athletes in training, through musicians and through students. Personally, I found the concept of neuroplasticity to be strange to get my head around at first. After dealing with chronic pain on and off, over a period spanning nearly 15 years, (with not much answers), I found myself in the chronic pain clinic after a really bad period and was introduced to this idea that I could retrain my brain's neural pathways to make new connections to strengthen, or overwrite my 'already learned' pain receptors. This was done mainly through building up a habit to gradually increase my activity levels, building up to where I am now.

Neuroplasticity = selective organising of connections between neurons in our brains

Researcher, and teacher, Judy Willis basically, summarised the near-exact neuroplasticity process, as when an activity is practiced (or a memory recalled) on a regular basis, the neural networks (electrochemical pathways) shape themselves according to that activity or memory. If practicing new things stops, the brain goes through a “pruning”, eventually eliminating that pathway. “Like in a system of freeways connecting various cities, the more cars going to certain destination, the wider the road that carries them needs to be. The fewer cars travelling that way, however, the fewer lanes are needed.”
“Cells that fire together, wire together” Performing tasks, or recalling information that causes different neurons to work simultaneously, strengthens those connections. This then creates “thick, hardy road maps” linking various parts of the brain. Stimulating one neuron will more than likely trigger the next one to fire. “Practice makes permanent. The more times the network is stimulated, the stronger and more efficient it becomes.” (Willis, "Mind, Brain & Education". 2010)


Changing Brains in the Classroom

Sharing this information with students will also have an affect on their brains - and mindset - too. Lisa Blackwell (Columbia University), along with Kali Trzesniewski and Carol Dweck (Stanford University), published a study in the journal Child Development in 2007 that found that both morale and grades took a leap when students understood that "intelligence is malleable". The students who already believed this of themselves did better in school, and also their classmates who had been taught the same concept by researchers. The results from the groups were significantly higher than those who had not been taught about neuroplasticity.
Willis had also observed that in her own middle school class, her students were more motivated to study and engage, because those students knew that they had the capability to "[build] knowledge and change their brains".

Tips for making malleable brains: (shared from Edutopia)

  • Practice, practice, practice. Repeating an activity, retrieving a memory, and reviewing material in a variety of ways helps build thicker, stronger, more hard-wired connections in the brain.
  • Put information in context. Recognizing that learning is, essentially, the formation of new or stronger neural connections, it makes sense to prioritise activities that help students tap into already-existing pathways. For example, combine subjects, or give students projects that allow them to create according to their lives. "Whenever new material is presented in such a way that students see relationships between concepts", writes Willis, "they generate greater brain cell activity and achieve more successful long-term memory storage and retrieval."
  • Let students know that this is how the brain works. Breaking through those neuro-mythological barriers that paint intelligence as predetermined may ease students' minds and encourage them to use their brains. Willis notes, "Especially for students who believe they are 'not smart,' the realization that they can literally change their brains through study and review is empowering."

Growth Mindset: The Power of Yet

Carol Dweck, known for her research on an individual’s implicit theory of intelligence, “In a fixed mindset, students believe their basic abilities, their intelligence, their talents, are just fixed traits. They have a certain amount and that’s that, and then their goal becomes to look smart all the time and never look dumb.

“In a Growth Mindset students understand that their talents and abilities can be developed through effort, good teaching and persistence. They don’t necessarily think everyone’s the same or anyone can be Einstein, but they believe everyone can get smarter if they work at it.”

In her Ted Talk, Carol mentioned that the best way to acknowledge someone’s successes or failures - or rather, in her example, children - was not to give praise for talent, gifts or for how many A’s they received, or for putting them down when their performance was unsatisfactory - but for how they took their journey. Having their effort, problem solving strategies acknowledged, and recognised as well as having their tried and failed attempts constructively guided did more to validate the mindset of “I can get there when I practice.” Dweck specifically gives it the name of praising wisely. It gives the feedback and praise more meaning for both parties, especially when that confidence is built from the basis of a growth mindset.
